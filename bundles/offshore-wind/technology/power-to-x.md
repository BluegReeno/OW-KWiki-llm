# Overview

Power-to-X (PtX) covers technologies that convert renewable electricity into another energy carrier — most commonly green hydrogen via electrolysis, and secondarily derivatives such as ammonia or synthetic fuels. Applied offshore, PtX lets a wind farm produce hydrogen instead of (or alongside) exporting electricity, which can avoid or reduce the need for costly [HVDC grid connection](hvdc-grid-connection.md) infrastructure, particularly for farms far from shore or in grid-constrained areas. As of 2026 the technology remains at demonstrator scale — commercial gigawatt-scale offshore PtX is not yet operating anywhere.

# Technical Pathways

Three main architectures are being pursued:

- **Offshore distributed hydrogen production**: electrolysers integrated into individual turbines or small clusters, producing hydrogen locally before piping it to shore. Siemens Energy's H2Mare initiative is developing turbines with integrated electrolysis, with a first prototype targeted for 2026.
- **Offshore centralized hydrogen production**: a dedicated offshore platform aggregates power from the array and runs large-scale electrolysis, similar in concept to an offshore substation. The Dolphyn project off Aberdeen and the HOPE project off Ostend (targeting a first 10 MW production unit in 2026) follow variants of this model.
- **Onshore hydrogen production**: power is exported conventionally via cable and electrolysis happens onshore, avoiding the added complexity of a marine electrolysis environment at the cost of retaining the export-cable requirement.

Producing hydrogen offshore requires either transporting seawater-derived freshwater for electrolysis or developing seawater electrolysis technology directly, which remains a significant materials and corrosion challenge.

# Economics and Rationale

Hydrogen (or ammonia) can be transported by pipeline or ship rather than cable, which changes the infrastructure trade-off for very remote or very large farms. However, round-trip efficiency losses from electrolysis mean PtX is generally viewed as a complement to direct electrification for hard-to-abate sectors (steel, shipping, fertilizer) rather than a wholesale replacement for grid export.
