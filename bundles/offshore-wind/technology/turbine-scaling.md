# Overview

Offshore wind turbine ratings have grown roughly tenfold in two decades, from ~3-5 MW machines in the early 2010s to commercial units of 14-16 MW and prototypes exceeding 20 MW by the mid-2020s. Larger turbines mean fewer units are needed for a given farm capacity, which cuts the number of foundations, inter-array cables, and installation/maintenance visits per MW — a major driver of offshore wind's steep cost decline over the past decade.

# Current State (2026)

- Commercial offshore turbines from major OEMs — [vestas](/companies/vestas.md), [siemens-gamesa](/companies/siemens-gamesa.md), [ge-vernova-offshore-wind](/companies/ge-vernova-offshore-wind.md) — are now clustered in the 14-18 MW range for projects reaching final investment decision, with rotor diameters exceeding 220-250 m and hub heights above 150-180 m.
- China's Dongfang Electric unveiled a 26 MW turbine in 2024 with a rotor diameter of roughly 340 m (comparable in span to twelve Boeing 747s), completing installation in 2025.
- A 20 MW-class turbine with a 300 m rotor was installed off Fujian, China in January 2026 — currently the largest turbine actually operating at sea.
- Larger rotors and hub heights push capacity factors up to roughly 55-65% in favorable sites, further improving the economics of scale.

# Economic Impact

Bigger turbines reduce balance-of-plant cost per MW: fewer foundations (see [fixed-bottom-foundations.md](fixed-bottom-foundations.md) and [floating-wind.md](floating-wind.md)), less inter-array cabling, and fewer [offshore substation](offshore-substations.md) connections and crane operations. This dynamic has been a central driver behind the cost reductions seen in projects like [hornsea-2](/projects/hornsea-2.md) and [dogger-bank](/projects/dogger-bank.md), both built with turbines in the 8-13 MW class at the time, versus earlier farms built with sub-5 MW units. Larger turbines also concentrate risk: fewer, larger single points of failure, and heavier logistics requirements (larger installation vessels, ports, blade-handling equipment) that not all supply chains can yet support.

# Scaling Limits

Industry analysis increasingly suggests turbine scaling is approaching physical and economic limits: blade tip speeds, material fatigue, transport and port constraints, and installation vessel crane capacity all become binding constraints beyond roughly 20-25 MW. Some forecasts suggest turbines installed in the mid-2030s will be similar in size to those installed in 2026, with future cost reductions coming more from serial manufacturing, reliability, and installation efficiency than from further scaling.
