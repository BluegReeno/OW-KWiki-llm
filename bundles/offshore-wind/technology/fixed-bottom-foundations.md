# Overview

Fixed-bottom foundations anchor a turbine tower rigidly to the seabed, either by piling, gravity, or a lattice of driven piles. They remain the dominant technology worldwide — essentially all commercial-scale offshore wind capacity built to date uses fixed-bottom structures — because they are cheaper and better understood than [floating platforms](floating-wind.md), but they are limited to water depths of roughly 60-80 m at the outer edge of economic viability.

# Foundation Types

- **Monopile**: a single large-diameter steel tube driven into the seabed. The simplest, most standardized, and lowest-cost design; used almost universally in water depths up to about 30-40 m and, with larger-diameter "XXL" monopiles, increasingly pushed toward 50 m+. It is the workhorse foundation of most North Sea and US East Coast farms, including projects like [vineyard-wind-1](/projects/vineyard-wind-1.md) and [empire-wind-1](/projects/empire-wind-1.md).
- **Jacket**: a multi-legged tubular steel lattice structure (typically 3-4 legs) driven into the seabed with smaller piles at each leg. More material- and installation-intensive than a monopile but far more rigid and weight-efficient at greater depth, making it the preferred solution once monopiles lose cost competitiveness — roughly the 30-60 m depth band. Widely used for offshore substation platforms as well as turbines.
- **Gravity-based structure (GBS)**: a large concrete or steel base resting on the seabed under its own weight, with no piling required. Best suited to shallow water (0-30 m) with stable, load-bearing seabed conditions; installation avoids pile-driving noise, which can matter for environmental permitting.

Multi-pile designs such as tripods are used less commonly, generally displaced by the jacket in the 30-60 m depth band.

# Depth Bands (typical)

| Foundation | Typical depth |
|---|---|
| Gravity-based | 0-30 m |
| Monopile | 0-40-50 m |
| Jacket | 30-60 m |
| Floating | 50-60 m+ |

Depth thresholds shift as monopile manufacturing and installation-vessel capability scale up; the monopile/jacket crossover point has moved deeper over the past decade as XXL monopiles have become feasible.
