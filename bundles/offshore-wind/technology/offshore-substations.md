# Overview

An offshore substation sits between the wind turbine array and the onward transmission link. Turbines feed medium-voltage AC (typically 66 kV) through subsea inter-array cables into the substation, where transformers step the voltage up — either to a higher AC voltage for [HVDC conversion](hvdc-grid-connection.md) or directly for HVAC export to shore. Large farms may need several substations: the [dogger-bank](/projects/dogger-bank.md) project and the UK's Outer Dowsing farm, for example, use multiple offshore transformer platforms plus separate reactive compensation platforms to manage the reactive power generated by long export cables.

# Structure and Equipment

A typical substation platform is a topside module mounted on a jacket foundation (see [fixed-bottom-foundations.md](fixed-bottom-foundations.md)) in shallow-to-medium depth, generally up to about 50-60 m. The topside houses:

- Power transformers, stepping voltage up from array collection voltage
- Switchgear for circuit protection and isolation
- Reactive power compensation equipment (needed to offset cable capacitance on long AC export runs)
- For HVDC-connected farms, the AC-side equipment feeds a separate converter platform rather than exporting AC directly

Platforms are increasingly delivered as prefabricated, factory-tested modules to reduce offshore commissioning time — Siemens Energy's Offshore Transformer Module (OTM) concept, deployed at the [Inch Cape](/projects/inch-cape.md) project on a 68 m jacket, is one example of this modular approach.

# Floating Substations

As arrays move into deeper water for [floating wind](floating-wind.md), fixed-jacket substations become uneconomical, driving development of floating substation platforms. These face added structural-integrity and standardisation challenges because the topside equipment — designed for a static structure — must now tolerate continuous motion from wind and waves.
