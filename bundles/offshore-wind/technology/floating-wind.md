# Overview

Floating offshore wind (FOW) supports a turbine on a buoyant hull anchored to the seabed with mooring lines, rather than a structure rigidly driven or embedded into it. It targets water depths beyond roughly 50-60 m, where [fixed-bottom foundations](fixed-bottom-foundations.md) become uneconomical or structurally unviable — opening up deep-water resources off the US West Coast, the Mediterranean, Japan, South Korea, and much of the Atlantic-facing UK coastline (e.g. [scotwind-leasing-round](/tenders/scotwind-leasing-round.md)). The technology is still pre-commercial-scale: most capacity worldwide is demonstrator or pilot projects, with the first multi-hundred-MW commercial farms only reaching FID in the mid-2020s.

# Platform Types

Three canonical hull archetypes dominate current development:

- **Spar-buoy**: a long, slender, deep-draft cylinder ballasted low to lower the centre of gravity. Simple and stable in open water but requires deep-water quayside assembly and towing. Equinor's Hywind concept is the most mature commercial demonstration of this design.
- **Semi-submersible**: a column-stabilized platform with a wide waterplane area, buoyant at the surface and held by catenary or taut moorings. Easier to port-assemble and tow than a spar, and the most widely adopted design for near-term commercial projects.
- **Tension Leg Platform (TLP)**: a buoyant hull held down by taut vertical tendons anchored to the seabed. TLPs constrain heave and pitch motion tightly, which reduces dynamic loads on the turbine and tower, but they need more complex, higher-precision mooring and anchoring installation. Research increasingly targets TLPs for intermediate-to-deep water (roughly 300-370 m).

Hybrid "semi-spar" concepts are also emerging, aiming to combine the shallower draft of a semi-submersible with the motion stability of a spar.

# Economics and Outlook

Floating wind remains significantly more expensive per MW than fixed-bottom, driven by mooring, dynamic (rather than static) export cables, and less mature supply chains and port infrastructure. Turbine ratings for floating applications are scaling in step with fixed-bottom units — commercial floating projects are increasingly designed around 15-20+ MW turbines (see [turbine-scaling.md](turbine-scaling.md)) to spread the fixed cost of the floater and moorings over more output. Cost declines are expected to follow a similar learning curve to early fixed-bottom wind, contingent on serial fabrication of floaters and dedicated installation vessels.
