# Overview

The **Bureau of Ocean Energy Management (BOEM)**, an agency of the US Department of the Interior, regulates offshore wind development on the Outer Continental Shelf (OCS) under authority granted by the Outer Continental Shelf Lands Act (OCSLA), as amended by the Energy Policy Act of 2005. BOEM oversees planning, competitive leasing, and environmental/technical review of offshore wind projects in federal waters, while the Bureau of Safety and Environmental Enforcement (BSEE) oversees safety and environmental compliance during construction and operations.

The leasing process runs through four broad phases: (1) Planning and Area Identification, where BOEM designates Wind Energy Areas (WEAs) from broader Call Areas; (2) Lease Sale, a competitive auction for development rights; (3) Site Assessment Plan (SAP), covering site surveys and resource assessment; and (4) Construction and Operations Plan (COP), BOEM's environmental (NEPA) and technical review leading to approval, modification, or denial of the project.

As of 2025–2026, the US offshore wind leasing and permitting framework has faced significant policy disruption under the Trump administration, including a leasing pause, mass de-designation of Wind Energy Areas, and stop-work orders on projects already under construction, several of which are being contested in federal court.

# Key Facts

- **Legal basis**: Outer Continental Shelf Lands Act (OCSLA), amended by the Energy Policy Act of 2005.
- **Regulator**: BOEM (Department of the Interior) for leasing/permitting; BSEE for safety and environmental enforcement during construction/operations.
- **Process phases**: Planning & Area Identification (Wind Energy Areas) → competitive Lease Sale → Site Assessment Plan (SAP) → Construction and Operations Plan (COP), with NEPA environmental review throughout.
- **20 January 2025**: presidential memorandum temporarily pausing new offshore wind leasing on the OCS pending a federal practices review.
- **30 July 2025**: BOEM announced cancellation of all designated Wind Energy Areas, de-designating over 3.5 million acres (Gulf of Maine, New York Bight, California, Oregon, Central Atlantic, Gulf of Mexico).
- **December 2025**: stop-work orders issued on five projects under construction — Coastal Virginia Offshore Wind Commercial, Empire Wind 1, Revolution Wind, Sunrise Wind, and Vineyard Wind 1 (a combined ~5.8 GW) — citing national security/radar interference concerns.
- **8 December 2025**: a federal judge (D. Mass.) ruled the January 2025 leasing-pause memorandum unlawful via summary judgment against the administration; legal challenges (including by Dominion Energy) continue.
- **Next scheduled lease sale**: a Gulf of Mexico offshore wind lease sale is planned for 2026, per BOEM/DOI.
- **30 June 2026**: DOI reached a settlement agreement with Duke Energy for Duke to exit its Carolina Long Bay lease offshore North Carolina — the fourth offshore wind lease buyout deal reached by the Trump administration as part of its broader push to unwind federal offshore wind leases.
